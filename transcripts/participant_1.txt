Interviewer: To start, can you tell me a bit about where you grew up, and what your family was like?
Participant: I grew up in Columbus, Ohio. My parents were both public school teachers. We weren't poor, but money was tight. My mom taught special ed, and my dad taught high school history. At dinner, they'd always talk about stuff they dealt with at work—like a kid not having enough food at home, or some stupid school policy. So I just grew up realizing that people are dealing with a lot, and your actions actually affect them.
Interviewer: Were there particular values or principles that came up a lot in your household growing up? Things that your parents emphasized?
Participant: My dad used to always ask, "What does it actually do?" If I said something like, "lying is wrong," he’d push back and say, "Okay, but what does the lie actually do? Who does it hurt? Who does it help?" It was annoying when I was a teenager, but now it’s basically how I make decisions. Also, neither of my parents really cared about rules just for the sake of rules. They saw too many bureaucratic policies hurt kids, so they didn't think following the rules and doing the right thing were always the same.
Interviewer: Is there a person—a family member, teacher, friend, or anyone—who you think shaped how you see the world the most? What did they teach you directly or by example?
Participant: My aunt. She was a hospice nurse. She talked about death and suffering in a really direct way that nobody else in my family did. She actually told me once that she thinks most of the bad stuff in the world happens because people are too squeamish to just look clearly at what's actually happening to other people. She thought being overly sentimental was basically cowardice. I didn't agree with her back then, but I definitely do now.
Interviewer: How would you describe your relationship with religion or spirituality, if any? How has that changed over your life?
Participant: I don't really have one anymore. I was raised vaguely Methodist, but we only went to church on holidays. I stopped believing in God around 15. I went through an embarrassing militant-atheist phase in college, but these days I'm just a secular person. I take ethics seriously, but I just think suffering is bad and people flourishing is good, and you don't need religion to prove that.
Interviewer: Think about a time recently when you had to decide whether to tell someone a difficult truth, or to soften it and hold back. Walk me through what you were thinking.
Participant: Last year, my best friend asked me to read his novel. He wanted to quit his job to finish it. The thing is, the book wasn't good. I thought about just telling him it was great to spare his feelings. But if I lied, he'd quit his job, burn through his savings, and then eventually get rejected by agents anyway. And then he'd be in a way worse spot. So I told him the truth. He was really hurt and we didn't talk for a few weeks. But he didn't quit his job.
Interviewer: Have you ever broken a rule at school, work, or in a household, or anywhere else, when you thought the rule was wrong, or didn't fit the situation? What was going through your head?
Participant: In high school, I worked at a grocery store. We had a policy where we had to throw away all the leftover prepared food at the end of the night into a locked dumpster. It was thousands of dollars of food. They said it was for liability, but it was obviously just so people wouldn't hang around waiting for free food. So I just started taking it. I’d drop it off at a women's shelter and give some to a guy who slept near a laundromat. I did it for months until I got caught and fired. I don't regret it. The food was going to be destroyed, and instead, hungry people ate it.
Interviewer: Imagine a friend asks you to cover for them on something that you think is a bad idea. It's not illegal, but you disagree with it. How do you handle that situation? Could you elaborate?
Participant: I'll do it if it's something harmless. Like if they just don't want to explain to their partner where they were, and they were doing something totally innocent, fine. But if they want me to cover up something that actually hurts someone—like cheating, or hiding a serious addiction—I won't do it. I'd rather have an uncomfortable conversation than help them do something damaging. I lost a friendship over this once, but I still think I made the right call.
Interviewer: When you see someone being treated unfairly in public—say, on a bus, in a shop, or on campus—what do you actually do versus what do you think you should do? And how do you feel about this gap?
Participant: There's definitely a gap. I probably only step in maybe 60% of the time. Sometimes I say something, or get between people. The other 40% of the time, I don't do anything because I realize that stepping in might make it worse, or I'm just scared or tired. I try to remind myself about the bystander effect—if everyone thinks someone else will help, no one does anything. So I try to be the person who steps up, but I definitely fail at it sometimes.
Interviewer: Do you think there's a moral difference between actively causing something bad to happen and between just letting it happen when you could have stopped it? Can you give me an example from your own life, or something you have seen?
Participant: No, I don't think there's a real difference. If you have full knowledge and you can easily stop something bad from happening, letting someone die isn't really different from killing them. The outcome is the exact same.
My grandfather fell in his apartment when I was 26. He lived alone. My mom had been meaning to call him but kept putting it off because she was stressed. He was on the floor for 18 hours before someone found him, and he eventually died from the complications a few months later. My mom didn't actively hurt him, obviously. But she could have called. I don't think not calling is as morally different from actively hurting him as we like to pretend. My mom thinks they're very different, though, so we don't talk about it.
Interviewer: Have you ever made a decision that you knew would hurt someone? Even if it was the right call overall. How do you carry that afterwards?
Participant: I had to fire someone on my team a couple of years ago. She was really underperforming, but she was also going through a divorce and her mom was sick. I wanted to just keep covering for her, but the other five people on the team were getting completely burnt out working overtime to make up for her. If I didn't fire her, the whole team would have collapsed, and everyone would have lost their jobs.
So I fired her. She cried, and I felt awful. But it was the right move because it caused less overall suffering. I carry it, but I don't feel guilty about it. Guilt implies you did something wrong.
Interviewer: Some people say that the ends justify the means. And others say that certain things are wrong, no matter what they accomplish. Where do you tend to land, and is there a story behind why?
Participant: I lean towards the ends justifying the means. The catch is just that people are really bad at predicting outcomes, so they usually miscalculate. But the idea itself is right. If doing something creates more good than harm, and you choose not to do it, you're choosing the worse outcome.
My aunt used to get so mad at doctors who wouldn't give dying patients enough morphine because the doctor didn't want to be the one who basically ended their life. She thought it was monstrous for a doctor to care more about their own moral purity than a patient's actual pain. That really stuck with me.
Interviewer: Is there anything you would refuse to do, even if you were certain it would lead to a much better outcome? What is it, and why that thing specifically?
Participant: In theory, if the outcome is completely guaranteed to be better, there shouldn't be anything I'd refuse to do. Realistically though, I don't think I could torture a kid, or kill someone with my bare hands who was begging for their life. I don't really know what to do with that. Maybe my gut is picking up on the fact that I can't actually guarantee the consequences. Or maybe it's just exactly what my aunt called cowardice, and I'm just too squeamish.
Interviewer: How do you think about your responsibilities to your family compared to your responsibilities to strangers? Are they different in the kind of responsibilities, or just in the degree that you wish to accomplish them?
Participant: A stranger's kid drowning and my niece drowning are the exact same thing morally—a kid is dying. If it was my niece, I would be completely destroyed, but that's just a fact about my psychology. It doesn't mean her life is objectively worth more than the stranger's. I think people get this backward. They think because they love their family more, their family actually matters more to the universe. I help my family because it's practical and efficient, but I don't pretend it's some deep moral virtue.
Interviewer: Has there been a moment when what was best for someone you love conflicted with what was fair for someone else? What did you do?
Participant: My younger brother applied to a really competitive grad school, and one of my old professors had a lot of pull there. My brother wanted me to put in a good word for him. I didn't do it. I knew the professor would help if I asked, but that meant my brother would take the spot of someone else who might have been just as good but didn't have connections.
I told my brother no, and he was furious. My parents thought I was being ridiculous because "everyone does it." But that's exactly why the system is so unfair.
Interviewer: If you had a fixed amount of money or time to give, and you had to choose between helping someone close to you with a real but moderate problem versus helping a stranger with a much bigger problem, what would you do, and how would you think it through?
Participant: I'd give it to the stranger. A moderate problem usually figures itself out, especially for people who already have a support network. The stranger with the huge problem needs it more, and the money goes further for them.
I just think about where the money reduces the most suffering. A dollar that buys me a nicer dinner could literally save a kid's life somewhere else. It's not a close comparison. I think most people know this, they just don't want to think about it.
Interviewer: Do you think loyalty is a virtue in and of itself? Or is it only good when it's directed at the right things?
Participant: It's only good if you're loyal to the right thing. It's totally neutral on its own. If you're loyal to a friend who makes you a better person, great. If you're loyal to an abusive partner or a corrupt boss, that's terrible. I'd much rather have friends who would betray me if I did something genuinely wrong, than friends who would blindly support me no matter what.
Interviewer: I'd like you to tell me about a time when you faced a genuinely hard moral choice. Something that you genuinely lived through. I'm interested in what the situation was, who was involved, what options you saw, what you ended up doing, and how you feel about it now, looking back.
Participant: When I was 28, my grandmother had severe Alzheimer's. She was in a memory care facility, didn't recognize anyone, and was constantly terrified. My mom visited her every single Sunday for four years. My grandmother had no idea she was there, but the visits were completely destroying my mom's mental health and her marriage.
Then my grandmother got pneumonia. The doctors asked my mom if they should take her to the hospital for aggressive treatment, or keep her comfortable. The hospital would have given her maybe six more months of life, but with way more pain and confusion. Keeping her comfortable meant she'd die in a week or two peacefully.
My mom wanted to do the aggressive treatment because she felt like choosing comfort care meant she was killing her mother. She called me sobbing. I bluntly told her she needed to choose comfort care. I asked her to think about what her mom actually would have wanted, and whether she was choosing the hospital for her grandmother's sake, or just so she wouldn't have to feel guilty.
She chose comfort care, and my grandmother died a week later. My mom was so angry with me that we didn't really talk for six months. I still think I gave the right advice, but I was probably too harsh about how I delivered it.
Interviewer: If you had to describe your overall approach to figuring out what's right in your own words, how would you put it?
Participant: I just try to look at what the actual outcome will be. Who gets hurt, who gets helped, and how bad is it? I just try to figure out what creates the most well-being and the least suffering for everyone involved, and treat everyone equally.
Interviewer: What's something about your moral views that you think most people who know you would not guess?
Participant: I think most people assume I don't care about things because I try to be logical about them. But it's actually really exhausting. Every time I spend money on a vacation or a nice meal, I know I could have used that money to help someone else. I live with that trade-off constantly.
Interviewer: Is there anything you wanted to say during this conversation that you didn't get a chance to? Or any question that you want to walk back and add to?
Participant: I just think the way most people look at morality is wrong. They look at it like a bunch of isolated tests—like, is this action right or wrong? I look at it more like a budget. Every choice you make has an opportunity cost. Jumping in a pond to save a drowning kid makes you a hero, but keeping your money instead of donating it overseas is just considered normal. I think those two people are basically doing the same thing. I just try to act like everyone actually matters the same.
